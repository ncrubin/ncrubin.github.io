Legendre Transforms

Date Fri 05 February 2016

While cleaning out my desk I came across a paper that was very helpful when taking thermodynamics my first year of graduate school. The paper is titled, "Making Sense of the Legendre Transform" and is written by Zia, Redish, and Mckay (AAPT, Arxiv). As the title suggests, the paper is pedagogical in nature and focuses on the mathematics and various uses of the Legendre transform. As a first year graduate student coming from a physical chemistry back ground I found the accessibility of the paper refreshing. The authors clearly connect the idea of encoding the information of a convex function by its derivative--i.e. that there is a one-to-one mapping of \(x\) to \(dF/dx\) for a convex function \(F(x)\). This idea summarized in Fig 3. of their paper.

\(F(x)\) is convex which means its slope (\(s(x) = \frac{d F}{d x}\)) is monotonic and Hessian is positive definite. Because there is a unique slope for each \(x\) there is a one-to-one correspondence between \(s\) and \(x\). This single-valued-ness is what makes the Legendre transform an exact encoding for convex functions. Given that the \(y\)-intercept is the new dual function whose dependent variable is the slope (\(s\)) it is obvious from the figure

\begin{align} s x = F(x(s)) + G(s) \end{align}

simply restating the definition of a derivative as rise-over-run. By rearranging, you arrive at the usual form of the Legendre transform

\begin{align} G(s) = sx - F(x(s)) \end{align}

From the above equations it is straightforward to show the Legendre transform is its own inverse, the extrema properties

\begin{align} F_{\mathrm{min}} = -G(0) \;\;\;\; G_{\mathrm{min}} = -F(0) \end{align}

, and an infinite set of relations between derivatives of the potentials and derivatives of their independent variable with respect to the dual variable.

Two of my favorite parts of the discussion were the basic examples of the relationship between the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian forms of classical equations of motion and the application to a simple harmonic potential. The highlight of the Lagrangian-to-Hamiltonian discussion is that it completely demystifies the appearance of the negative sign for the potential when going from Hamiltonian to Lagrangian equations of motion. The harmonic potential problem is great because it perfectly highlights how one can use the idea of using the derivative of a potential (the force) to encode the same information of the system. This idea is especially important for appreciating the various thermodynamic potentials which are derived later on in the paper.
